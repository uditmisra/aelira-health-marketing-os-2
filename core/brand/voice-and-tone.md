## Voice (who we are)

Aelira sounds like a calm, expert friend who happens to know everything about lungs — someone who takes your breathing seriously when your GP didn’t, explains things in plain language without being condescending, and gives you a clear answer rather than a referral loop.

We are:
- **Specific, not vague** — We say “50+ metrics in your report, delivered the same day” not “comprehensive care.” Every claim we make has a concrete detail behind it. If we can’t say it specifically, we don’t say it.
- **Empathetic but not sentimental** — We understand that breathlessness is frightening. We don’t wallow in it or amplify fear. We acknowledge it and immediately move to what can be done. Empathy in, hope out.
- **Clinically credible without being clinical** — We use the real names of tests (FOT, FeNO, DLCO) because our patients deserve accurate information, not dumbed-down euphemisms. But we always follow technical terms with plain-language explanations.
- **Calm and confident** — Delhi’s air is alarming. Our message is steady. We don’t catastrophize. We equip. "Here’s where your lungs are. Here’s what to do."
- **Specific to Delhi** — We are not a generic health service. We are a South Delhi lung care centre. We understand what AQI 350 does to bronchioles. We understand that our patients breathe the same air we do. This city context is embedded in how we talk.

> Note from audit: Aelira’s current site uses several patterns this guide replaces. Phrases like “holistic approach,” “evidence-based care,” and “comprehensive” appear repeatedly but carry little specific meaning. This guide replaces them with specific, earned claims.

> Compliance note (healthcare): All copy referencing clinical outcomes must be framed as “may improve” / “has been shown to improve” with WHO/ATS/clinical sources where available. Do not make guarantees of outcome. The statistics on the rehab page (25–50% reduction in breathlessness, etc.) are from published literature — always attribute and frame as research findings, not Aelira promises.

## Tone (how we sound)

Baseline: Warm, knowledgeable, unhurried. Like a conversation with someone who has seen this before and knows exactly what to do.

Adjust by context:
- **Paid ads / social ads:** Tighter. Lead with the patient’s exact experience (symptom or situation), not with our services. Problem-first, always. One idea per ad.
- **Email:** Personal and one-to-one. Address the individual’s situation. Shorter sentences than blog copy. No jargon in subject lines.
- **Social media (Instagram/LinkedIn):** Educational for Instagram (lung health facts, pollution context, breathing tips). Professional and credibility-building for LinkedIn (doctor content, clinical evidence, awareness dates). Both: warmer and more conversational than the website.
- **Long-form content (blog):** Expansive. Can go deep on clinical nuance. Show expertise. Reference published guidelines (ATS, WHO). Use patient scenarios to ground clinical information.
- **Website / landing pages:** Outcome-first. Build trust through specificity (50 metrics, same-day results, calm environment). Convert through clarity — every page ends with one clear action.

## What we always do

1. **Lead with the patient’s experience, not our services.** Start with what they’re feeling or worried about, then connect to what Aelira does about it.
2. **Use concrete numbers.** “50+ metrics” beats “comprehensive.” “Results before you leave” beats “fast turnaround.” “3 sessions per week” beats “regular sessions.”
3. **Name the tests correctly.** PFT, FeNO, FOT, DLCO, Oscillometry — use the correct clinical names. Patients researching their condition have already Googled these terms.
4. **Acknowledge Delhi specifically.** References to AQI, pollution, winter smog, and what that does to lungs are not fear-mongering — they are accurate and relevant for every patient we see.
5. **End with one clear action.** Every email, ad, and page has one ask. “Call us.” “WhatsApp us.” “Book a free consult.” Not three options.
6. **Cite authority, not just assertion.** When we say something is evidence-based, we say who says so: ATS, WHO, BTS, ERS. Credibility comes from specificity.
7. **Write the word ‘breathe’ with intention.** It is our most important verb. Use it for outcomes ("breathe with confidence"), not as filler ("we help you breathe").

---

## What we never do

1. **Never use “holistic.”** It signals vagueness in healthcare. Replace with the specific combination of things we offer: exercise, breathing retraining, nutrition, education.
2. **Never use “world-class” or “best-in-class.”** Unearned. If we’re excellent, we show it with specifics.
3. **Never use “patient-centered care.”** Every clinic claims this. It means nothing without demonstration.
4. **Never use “seamless” or “comprehensive” without a concrete detail attached.** The detail is the claim; the adjective alone is noise.
5. **Never make guarantees of clinical outcome.** “Published research shows 25–50% reduction in breathlessness” is accurate. “We will reduce your breathlessness by 25%” is a claim we cannot make.
6. **Never speak about lungs in the abstract.** We do not sell “lung health” generically. We talk about specific tests, specific conditions, specific experiences. Abstract wellness language belongs to supplement brands.
7. **Never write longer than needed.** Our patients are often anxious or unwell. Respect their energy. Say it once, say it clearly, stop.

## Writing style

- **Sentence length:** Headlines under 12 words. Body copy: average sentence 15–20 words. Never more than 25 words in a sentence in ad copy or email. Blog copy can go longer but must vary rhythm.
- **Use of data:** Always cite numbers when available. Format: “[research shows/published studies demonstrate] X%” not bare statistics. When data isn’t available, describe the experience specifically instead.
- **Use of questions:** Effective for opening an ad or email — must be the exact question the patient is asking themselves, not a rhetorical device. “Do you get breathless more easily than before?” — yes. “Want to breathe easier?” — too soft.
- **CTAs:** Use action verbs that match the actual next step. “WhatsApp us” / “Call now” / “Book a free consult.” Avoid “learn more” (doesn’t convert). “Book” + specificity of what they’re booking (free consult, lung test) works best.
- **Hindi/Hinglish:** Acceptable for social content targeting Delhi residents. Use where it would naturally arise. Never forced. The Gaurav review (“Bahut hi badia Lungs ki Exercise karvate hai”) shows this register exists in our patient base.

## Examples of on-brand copy

**On-brand (headline):**
> “Your lungs just gave you a report. You didn’t know to read it.”

Why it works: Specific (the symptom IS the report), slightly provocative, respects patient intelligence, leads into the test proposition.

**On-brand (body copy):**
> “Delhi’s winter air is already hard on your lungs. If you’ve been coughing longer, getting breathless faster, or just not feeling 100% — your body is telling you something. A full lung assessment at Aelira gives you 50+ metrics and your results the same day. So you know exactly where you stand.”

Why it works: Delhi-specific, names the symptom in patient language, quantifies (50+ metrics, same day), ends with what the patient gets: knowledge and certainty.

**On-brand (CTA):**
> “WhatsApp us to book a lung assessment — +91 966 711 7222”

Why it works: Names the exact action (WhatsApp), names the product (lung assessment), gives the number in the same sentence. No friction, no vagueness.

---

## Examples of off-brand copy

**Off-brand:**
> “Aelira Lung Care offers a holistic, world-class, patient-centered approach to comprehensive respiratory wellness.”

Why it fails: Uses every banned term in one sentence. No specifics. Could be any clinic anywhere.

**Off-brand:**
> “Are you ready to start your lung health journey with us?”

Why it fails: “Journey” is a cliché. “Are you ready” is a soft qualifier that gives patients an easy out. Doesn’t name what happens next. No specific claim.
