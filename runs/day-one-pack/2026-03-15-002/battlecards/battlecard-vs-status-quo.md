# Battlecard: Aelira vs. Doing Nothing (Status Quo)

**Built by:** competitive-landscape-builder / battlecard agent
**Run ID:** 2026-03-15-002
**For use by:** Patient coordinators; useful for WhatsApp conversations where patient is hesitant

---

## When this card applies
A patient says:
- "My GP said it's probably nothing."
- "It's just the pollution / winter / getting older."
- "I'll come in if it gets worse."
- "I'm not that breathless, just a bit."

---

## The One-Sentence Answer
"Your GP is right that mild breathlessness usually isn't an emergency. The question is whether you want to know your baseline now — so you have something to compare against if it does get worse."

---

## Talking Points

**The baseline argument:**
> "The most useful thing a lung function test does when you have mild symptoms is establish your baseline. If you come back in a year and something has changed, we have something to measure against. If you never get tested, we're guessing."

**The early detection argument:**
> "Early-stage COPD and pollution-related small airway damage often produce no obvious symptoms for years. By the time breathlessness becomes significant enough that most patients seek help, lung function has often declined substantially. A test now, when you're 'not that breathless,' is the one that can change what happens later."

**For post-COVID hesitation:**
> "Many post-COVID patients are told their symptoms are anxiety or fatigue. A FeNO test can show whether there's real airway inflammation present — the kind that responds to treatment. If it's physical, knowing that changes your options. If the test is normal, that information is also valuable."

**For the 'I'll come if it gets worse' patient:**
> "Absolutely — your call. If you do decide to come, our first consultation is free for rehab enquiries, and a WhatsApp conversation costs nothing. We'll help you figure out if testing makes sense for your situation before you commit to anything."

---

## The key insight
The status quo patient isn't wrong — they're appropriately not panicking. The goal isn't to create fear. It's to shift the frame from "emergency" to "intelligent preventive care" — especially for someone living in Delhi.
