# Blog Post 1
## You've Had a PFT. But Did It Actually Tell You Enough?

*Category: Diagnostics | Reading time: 5 min*

Most people who've had a pulmonary function test in Delhi walked away with a printout, a few circled numbers, and a vague sense that either "everything's fine" or "come back if it gets worse."

That's not what a comprehensive lung assessment looks like.

Here's what a standard spirometry test — the one most clinics and diagnostic labs perform — actually measures: airflow in and out of your lungs, and how fast. The two primary values are FVC (forced vital capacity) and FEV1 (the volume you can exhale in the first second). These are useful. They're not complete.

**What spirometry doesn't catch**

The small airways — the bronchioles deep in your lungs — are where a significant amount of early-stage lung disease begins. Standard spirometry doesn't measure small airway function with precision. By the time small airway damage shows up on a basic spirometry report, it's been progressing for a while.

This is where Lung Oscillometry (also called the FOT test — Forced Oscillation Technique) becomes important. It measures airway resistance in both large and small airways by sending gentle sound waves through your lungs while you breathe normally. No forced exhalation required. No "blow as hard as you can." You just breathe.

FOT is particularly valuable for:
- Detecting early-stage small airway disease before symptoms appear
- Patients who can't perform forced breathing manoeuvres (elderly, young children, post-surgery)
- Distinguishing asthma from COPD when spirometry results are ambiguous

Another gap: the FeNO test. If you've ever been told you "might have asthma" or "possibly COPD" without a definitive answer, there's a reason — spirometry alone can't tell them apart. The FeNO test measures fractional exhaled nitric oxide, a direct marker of eosinophilic airway inflammation. Elevated FeNO = asthma is the more likely diagnosis. Normal FeNO = COPD or non-inflammatory disease is more likely. This matters because the treatment is completely different.

And DLCO — the diffusing capacity of the lungs for carbon monoxide — tells you how effectively oxygen crosses from your lungs into your bloodstream. This is what gets impaired in interstitial lung disease (ILD), pulmonary fibrosis, and post-COVID lung damage. A spirometry result might look near-normal while your DLCO has dropped significantly.

If you've been told your spirometry was "normal" but you're still getting breathless more easily than before, consider whether you've actually had a comprehensive lung assessment — or just a standard one.

# Blog Post 2
## Delhi's Air Is What It Is. Here's What It's Actually Doing to Your Lungs.

*Category: Delhi & Lung Health | Reading time: 6 min*

Every winter, the headlines return: Delhi's AQI exceeds 300. Schools close. Masks sell out. And then the season passes, the air (slightly) clears, and most people go back to not thinking about their lungs until next October.

Here's what the headlines don't tell you.

**The damage isn't seasonal**

Particulate matter — specifically PM2.5, the fine particles that penetrate deep into the alveoli — doesn't just irritate your airways during the winter spikes. Chronic, year-round exposure at Delhi's baseline PM2.5 levels (well above WHO guidelines even in "good" months) causes cumulative, progressive lung changes.

A 2025 study in *Scientific Reports* found that even on average pollution days in Delhi, respiratory deposition doses of PM2.5 exceeded WHO safe benchmarks by up to 40-fold during evening commutes. This isn't a seasonal problem. It's a daily one.

**Who's at risk — and it's broader than you think**

The high-risk categories most people know: smokers, the elderly, people with existing COPD or asthma. What most people don't know:

- **Non-smokers** are developing lung cancer at rising rates in Delhi, driven by pollution exposure. Nearly one in three lung cancer patients in India today has never smoked.
- **Children** breathe faster and their lungs are still developing, making them more vulnerable per unit of exposure than adults.
- **Young adults without symptoms** may already have measurable small airway changes that won't manifest as obvious breathlessness for years.

Dr. Harsh Vardhan Puri, chest surgeon at Medanta, described Delhi's air this way in a January 2026 interview: "Breathing Delhi air is equivalent to smoking nearly 25 cigarettes a day."

**What a lung assessment actually shows**

This is where knowing your lung function baseline becomes a medical asset, not just a wellness exercise.

A comprehensive assessment — PFT, FeNO, Lung Oscillometry, and DLCO — establishes your current lung function across multiple dimensions. Small airway integrity. Airway inflammation. Gas transfer efficiency. Airflow resistance.

For someone without symptoms, this baseline is what allows you to detect early change in future assessments. For someone with symptoms, it gives you the diagnostic picture that determines whether lifestyle changes are sufficient or treatment is needed.

**Timing matters**

The best time to get a lung assessment is before significant symptoms appear. The second-best time is now.

## What Is Pulmonary Rehabilitation — and Why Your Pulmonologist Keeps Mentioning It

*Category: Rehabilitation | Reading time: 7 min*

If you've been diagnosed with COPD, ILD, severe asthma, or if you're recovering from a significant respiratory illness — including COVID-19 — there's a strong chance your pulmonologist has said something about pulmonary rehabilitation.

And there's a similarly strong chance you nodded, received a pamphlet, and quietly didn't follow up on it.

Here's what it actually is — and why it's not optional if you want to feel significantly better.

**The problem it solves**

Breathlessness creates a cycle. When you feel short of breath with exertion, the natural response is to move less — take the lift instead of the stairs, stop walking to the market, skip the evening walk. This feels like safety. Physically, it's the opposite.

When muscles aren't used, they decondition. Deconditioned muscles require more oxygen to do less work. That means your lungs have to work harder for every movement. Which makes you more breathless. Which makes you move less.

Pulmonary rehabilitation breaks this cycle with a structured, supervised programme that:

1. **Rebuilds physical capacity** through progressive, monitored exercise — starting at whatever level you can manage, increasing as your capacity grows
2. **Trains breathing efficiency** — specific techniques (pursed-lip breathing, diaphragmatic breathing) that reduce the effort of each breath
3. **Addresses muscle conditioning** — particularly the respiratory muscles and the legs, which carry the highest oxygen demand during movement
4. **Supports airway clearance** — for conditions like bronchiectasis where mucus accumulation is a primary problem
5. **Provides disease education and medication optimisation** — inhaler technique training, recognizing exacerbation warning signs early
6. **Includes nutritional counselling** — because body weight significantly affects breathing effort and oxygen demand

**What the evidence says**

Pulmonary rehabilitation is not an experimental approach. It is recommended as a core treatment component by the American Thoracic Society, the British Thoracic Society, the European Respiratory Society, and the World Health Organization.

Published clinical studies document:
- 25–50% reduction in breathlessness during daily activities
- 15–35% improvement in walking distance
- 25–50% reduction in COPD-related hospital visits
- 20–40% improvement in quality of life scores
- Proven to increase life expectancy by 2–6 years in COPD and 2–10 years in ILD

These are not marginal gains. For someone who currently can't climb stairs without stopping, this represents the difference between dependence and independence.

**Why most people don't complete it**

The most common reason pulmonary rehabilitation goes unstarted or incomplete: it requires in-person attendance 3 times per week, consistently, over 6–12 weeks. In a hospital setting, this means fitting your schedule around OPD timings, fighting for a physiotherapy slot, and doing your rehab in a general ward alongside patients recovering from different conditions.

**Who should consider pulmonary rehabilitation**

Any patient with COPD (particularly MRC dyspnoea grade 2 or above), ILD, severe asthma, bronchiectasis, post-COVID lung impairment, or anyone recovering from a major respiratory illness. If you're getting breathless during activities you used to manage easily, the conversation with a specialist is worth having.
