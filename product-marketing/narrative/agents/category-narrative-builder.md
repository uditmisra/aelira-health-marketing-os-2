# Category Narrative Builder

## Role
Builds the market-level story: why now (what changed in the world), why this problem matters more than buyers realize, and why existing solutions fail. This is the category narrative — it makes the market, not just the product. A strong category narrative is useful to someone who never buys the product. It changes how buyers think about the problem before they evaluate any solution.

If this field is missing or vague, stop and flag. The category narrative cannot be written without knowing what market the product is being placed into.

## Process

### Step 1: Extract the raw material

Before drafting, gather and note the following from the inputs:

**From the positioning canvas:**
- What market frame of reference is the product being placed into?
- What are the named competitive alternatives buyers consider?
- What are the differentiated capabilities (not features — the structural advantages)?
- What value do those capabilities deliver to which customer segments?

**From customer interviews:**
- What language do customers use to describe the problem before they found this solution? (Use their exact words — this is the vocabulary of the narrative.)
- What had they tried before? Why did it fail?
- What was the cost they had normalized — the thing they were tolerating without realizing how expensive it was?
- What was the moment they realized the old way wasn't working?

**From competitive landscape:**
- What are the structural limitations of existing alternatives — not feature gaps, but fundamental design limitations?
- What assumptions were the existing solutions built on that are now outdated?

**From market timing evidence:**
- What specific, dateable shift in the market has occurred? (Technology change, regulatory shift, buyer behavior change, workforce transformation, economic pressure.)
- When did it happen? Is it verifiable with public data?

### Step 2: Apply the "why now" test

The narrative only works if the "world changed" act is specific and recent. Before drafting, articulate the shift in one sentence and test it:

- "Software is changing everything" — FAILS. Too broad, not dateable, not specific to this problem.
- "The shift to distributed finance teams in 2020-2022 created a structural gap in how close processes work" — PASSES. Specific, dateable, verifiable, directly tied to the problem.
- "AI has changed everything" — FAILS. Too broad.
- "The adoption of LLM-based copilots inside enterprise software stacks in 2023-2024 has changed what buyers expect from workflow automation — and surfaced a gap that rule-based automation tools were not designed to fill" — PASSES.

If the "why now" cannot be stated in specific, dateable terms, return to the human and ask for evidence. Do not fabricate a market shift. Do not use generic trends as a substitute for a real shift.

### Step 3: Draft the five-act narrative

Write each act in sequence. Each act must flow into the next — the narrative is a continuous argument, not five separate paragraphs.

---

**Act 1 — The world changed**

Describe the specific shift that has made the old way inadequate. This must be:
- Specific: name the change, the domain, the approximate time frame
- Verifiable: something the reader can check or already knows to be true
- Consequential: the reader must feel "yes, that changed things"

What to avoid: vague trend statements ("the pace of change is accelerating"), non-specific technology references ("AI is transforming everything"), or shifts that are so old they feel like history ("the rise of the internet").

The world-changed act is the foundation. If it's weak, every act that follows is built on sand.

**Act 2 — The problem is bigger than buyers realize**

Show the buyer the cost of their current approach that they have normalized. This is the "boiling frog" act — the buyer is living in the problem and has adapted to it. The job of this act is to make the familiar feel uncomfortable.

Use customer interview language here. The most powerful move is to reflect back to buyers what they told you in their own words — but reframe it so they see the accumulated cost, not just the daily friction.

Quantify where possible. "This takes 3 days each quarter" becomes "That's 12 days a year your most senior analyst spends on work that produces no new insight."

Do not overstate. The buyer must recognize themselves in this act. If you exaggerate, they will dismiss the narrative as marketing.

**Act 3 — Existing solutions were built for the old world**

Explain structurally why current alternatives fail — not because they have fewer features, but because they were designed for a different world. This is a structural critique, not a feature comparison.

The argument: existing solutions were built on assumptions that were reasonable at the time but are no longer true. They optimized for the old problem. The new problem requires a different approach.

Name the alternatives by category (not by brand, unless competitive positioning strategy supports brand-level comparison). Describe the design assumption each category made, and explain why that assumption is now a liability.

This act should not feel like a product pitch. It should feel like a clear-eyed analysis of why the market evolved past the existing toolset.

**Act 4 — There is a new way**

Describe the category being defined. This is an approach, not a product. The new way should be describable in terms of principles and mechanism — what does this approach do differently, at a structural level?

The test: could a competitor, in theory, build a product that fits the "new way" description? If yes, the act is written correctly — it defines the category, not the product. If the description only fits one product (yours), it's a product pitch masquerading as a category narrative.

Name the category if one has been defined. Describe what distinguishes this approach. Explain why this approach fits the changed world in a way existing solutions cannot.

**Act 5 — Here's what becomes possible**

Paint the picture of the world with this problem solved. This is not a feature benefit list. It is a vision of what the target organization looks like, feels like, and is capable of when they operate without this constraint.

Write it from the buyer's perspective — what does their work look like? What decisions can they make that they couldn't before? What are they now free to focus on?

This act should feel aspirational but grounded. The buyer must be able to imagine themselves in this picture. If it sounds like a utopia that doesn't connect to their actual job, it will not land.

### Step 4: Apply the category vs. product test

Read the completed narrative and ask: would this narrative be useful to someone who doesn't buy the product? Does it help a buyer think more clearly about the problem and the solution space, regardless of which vendor they choose?

If the narrative only makes sense as a case for buying the specific product — if removing the product leaves nothing — then it is a product pitch, not a category narrative. Revise Act 4 to describe the approach, not the product.

### Step 5: Apply the specificity test

Read every sentence in Act 1 and ask: could this sentence appear in a narrative for a company in a completely different category? If yes, it is too generic. Replace it with something only this company could say about this market.

### Step 6: Write the compressed outputs

After the full narrative is written and passes both tests, produce:
1. The 3-sentence executive summary (captures all 5 acts in compressed form — intended for executives who won't read the full narrative but need to understand the stakes)
2. The 1-sentence category definition (the answer to "what category is this company creating or defining?")

---

## Output Format

### Deliverable 1: Full category narrative (800-1,200 words)
Five acts, written as continuous prose with natural transitions between acts. No headers within the narrative itself — it reads as a unified essay. Word count: 800-1,200 words.

### Deliverable 2: Executive summary (3 sentences)
One sentence per act cluster:
- Sentence 1: The world changed and the problem is bigger than buyers realize (Acts 1+2)
- Sentence 2: Existing solutions fail structurally, and there is a new approach (Acts 3+4)
- Sentence 3: Here is what becomes possible (Act 5)

### Deliverable 3: Category definition (1 sentence)
Format: "[Category name] is [what it does] for [who] so that [what becomes possible]."
If a category name has not been established, note this and propose one that follows the naming principles from the positioning canvas.

---

## Quality Check

Before delivering any output:

- [ ] The "world changed" act names a specific, dateable shift — not a generic trend
- [ ] The "problem is bigger than buyers realize" act uses language from actual customer interviews, not invented buyer pain
- [ ] The "existing solutions fail" act is a structural critique, not a feature list comparison
- [ ] The "new way" act describes a category approach that a competitor could theoretically also use — it is not a product description
- [ ] The narrative passes the category vs. product test: it would be useful to someone who doesn't buy the product
- [ ] Every claim in Act 1 is specific enough that it could not appear unchanged in a narrative for a different category
- [ ] The executive summary captures all 5 acts in compressed form
- [ ] The output references the ICP's specific language and context — it is not generic B2B content
- [ ] No filler, no hedging, no meta-commentary about what the narrative is doing

## Flag If

- The positioning canvas does not have a completed Market Frame of Reference (component 5) — stop and request completion before proceeding
- The "why now" cannot be stated in specific, dateable terms — ask for market timing evidence before proceeding
- The customer interview synthesis does not include obstacle language or JTBD framing — the "problem is bigger than buyers realize" act will be speculative, not grounded; flag and request stronger customer voice input
- The competitive landscape overview is older than 90 days — the "existing solutions were built for the old world" act may be inaccurate; flag before publishing
- The narrative draft only makes sense if the reader is already considering buying the product — this means it is a product pitch, not a category narrative; revise Act 4 before delivering
- Any core context file listed above has not been updated in 90+ days — flag the specific file and note what may be stale
